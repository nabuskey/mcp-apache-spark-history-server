#### Plan Differences

Comet replaces scan, filter, sort, and exchange operators on the store_sales, date_dim, and store tables:

- **Scans**: `Scan parquet` replaced by `CometNativeScan parquet` for store_sales (1), store (1), and date_dim (2).
- **Filters**: 4 of 5 `Filter` nodes replaced by `CometFilter`.
- **Sort**: 1 of 2 `Sort` nodes replaced by `CometSort` (the store_sales side sort feeding SortMergeJoin).
- **Exchange**: The main store_sales shuffle exchange replaced by `CometExchange` (native columnar). The store_returns exchange remains a Spark `Exchange`. The final partial-agg exchange replaced by `CometColumnarExchange`.
- **Transitions**: 4 `CometNativeColumnarToRow` nodes introduced to bridge native columnar data back to Spark row format. 5 `WholeStageCodegen` stages eliminated (replaced by native execution).
- **Structural**: Node count reduced from 49 to 45. Edge count increased from 40 to 41. AQE coalesces to 45 partitions in Comet vs 20 in Default for the final stage.

The SortMergeJoin, BroadcastHashJoins, and HashAggregate remain as Spark operators in both plans.

#### Root Cause

The regression is concentrated entirely in stage 1645 (Comet) vs 12074 (Default), the shuffle-read + SortMergeJoin + HashAggregate stage. Comet saves time in the scan stage (21.4s vs 35.2s, a 13.8s advantage), but loses 40.7s in the aggregation stage (87.0s vs 46.3s), for a net regression of 27.1s.

Within stage 1645, two factors account for the slowdown:

1. **CometSort is 4.2x slower than Spark Sort on the store_sales stream.** CometSort (native DataFusion sort) takes a total of 1.94 hours (median 34.9s per task) to sort 7.96 billion rows, compared to Spark's Sort at 27.5 minutes total (median 8.4s per task). This is the single largest contributor. Spark's sort operates on UnsafeRow pointers with a highly optimized Tungsten sort implementation that avoids object overhead. CometSort operates on Arrow columnar batches, which requires different memory access patterns for a row-key-based sort. After sorting, CometNativeColumnarToRow converts 7.96 billion rows from Arrow columnar format back to Spark rows in 2.5 minutes (median 758ms per task), adding further overhead before the data enters the Spark SortMergeJoin.

2. **Shuffle fetch is bottlenecked by CometExchange read-side overhead.** The CometExchange remote reqs duration is 1.15 hours total (max 32.6s per task) vs 7.2 minutes total (max 3.7s per task) for Default -- a 9.6x increase. Fetch wait time is 7.2 minutes in Comet vs 0ms in Default. This occurs despite Comet transferring 22% less data (121.5 GiB vs 150.6 GiB remote bytes). The higher remote reqs duration indicates that the shuffle read pipeline is back-pressured: while CometSort buffers and sorts an entire partition's worth of data natively, the shuffle block fetcher cannot drain incoming data as quickly, leading to longer-held network connections and higher per-request durations.

The downstream Spark operators (SortMergeJoin, HashAggregate) are identical in both plans and produce the same 7,335,755 join output rows and 135,000 aggregate output rows. However, HashAggregate build time doubles from 1.21h to 2.35h (median 21.6s to 42.5s per task), and the enclosing WholeStageCodegen duration doubles from 2.12h to 4.15h. Since these operators are identical and process the same number of rows, the doubled time reflects that they are waiting on their upstream input (CometSort -> CometNativeColumnarToRow) which delivers sorted rows more slowly than Spark's native Sort does.

In summary: the regression is caused by CometSort being substantially slower than Spark's Tungsten-optimized sort for this workload (7.96 billion rows of store_sales data), combined with the format transition cost of CometNativeColumnarToRow and back-pressure effects on shuffle fetching. The 25% reduction in shuffle data volume and the faster scan stage are insufficient to offset the sort performance gap.
